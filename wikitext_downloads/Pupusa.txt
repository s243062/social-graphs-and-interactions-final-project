A '''pupusa''' is a thick [[Griddle#Residential|griddle]] cake or [[flatbread]] from [[El Salvador]] and <!-- Any change to these countries should be discussed on talk page first -->[[Honduras]]<ref name="Univision" /> made with [[cornmeal]] or [[rice flour]] stuffed with one or more ingredients including cheese, beans, {{lang|es|[[chicharrón]]}}, or squash. It can be served with {{lang|es|[[curtido]]}} and [[tomato sauce]] and is traditionally eaten by hand. Pupusas have origins in [[Pre-Columbian era|Pre-Columbian]] [[Mesoamerica]] but were first mentioned in 1837 by Guatemalan poet [[José Batres Montúfar]]. In El Salvador, the pupusa is the [[national dish]]<ref name="LPG">{{cite web |last1=Erazo |first1=Ronald |date=12 November 2023 |title=Las Pupusas: ¿Hubo Riña con Honduras? Cuál es el Origen de Este Alimento Prehispánico que Evolucionó Hasta ser Declarado Plato Nacional de El Salvador |trans-title=Pupusas: Was There a Dispute with Honduras? What is the Origin of this Pre-Hispanic Food that Evolved Until Being Declared the National Dish of El Salvador? |url=https://www.laprensagrafica.com/salvadorenisimo/Las-pupusas-un-alimento-de-la-epoca-prehispanica-que-evoluciono-hasta-ser-declarado-plato-nacional-de-El-Salvador-hubo-rina-con-Honduras-20231110-0061.html |access-date=9 July 2025 |work=[[La Prensa Gráfica]] |language=es}}</ref> and has a day to celebrate it.

== Etymology ==
The origin of the term ''pupusa'' is unknown. The ''{{ill|Dictionary of Americanisms (Royal Spanish Academy){{!}}Dictionary of Americanisms|es|Diccionario de americanismos}}'', published by the [[Association of Academies of the Spanish Language]], states that ''pupusa'' derives from the [[Nawat language|Nawat]] word (spoken by the [[Pipil people]]) {{lang|ppl|puxahua}} meaning "fluffy" or "fluffy thing".<ref>{{cite book|title=Diccionario de Americanismos|year=2010|trans-title=Dictionary of Americanisms|language=es|publisher=[[Association of Academies of the Spanish Language]]|isbn=9788429495508|oclc=535494434}}</ref>{{Rp|1787}} In Lidia Pérez de Novoa's book ''Interlude and Other Verses'', she believed that ''pupusa'' derives from the Nawat word {{lang|ppl|pupusawa}} meaning "to puff up".<ref name="Perez de Novoa">{{cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=BL-PDwAAQBAJ|title=Interludio y Otros Versos|trans-title=Interlude and Other Verses|language=es|date=2019|access-date=6 July 2023|first1=Lidia Georgina|last1=Pérez de Novoa|publisher=Liber Factory Coedición Lord Byron|isbn=9788417707392|oclc=1110126677}}</ref>{{Rp|71}} Ricardo Ernesto Roque, a professor at the [[Central American University, San Salvador|Central American University]] in [[San Salvador]], supported this etymology.<ref name="Univision" />

Salvadoran linguist Jorge Lemus argued that the word ''pupusa'' does not have Nawat roots, stating that the Pipil people referred to pupusas as {{lang|ppl|kukumuzin}}. In {{ill|Santiago Barberena{{!}}Santiago Barberena's|es|Santiago Ignacio Barberena}} book ''Quicheísmos: Contribution to the Study of American Folklore...'', he believed that the word ''pupusa'' originated from a combination of the [[Kʼicheʼ language|K'iche']] words {{lang|quc|pop}} (meaning "sphere") and {{lang|quc|utz}} (meaning "good thing"), forming the word {{lang|quc|poputz}} meaning "good sphere"; however, the term {{lang|quc|poputz}} does not appear in any K'iche' language dictionaries.<ref name="Lemus">{{cite web|url=https://www.researchgate.net/publication/339887120|title=La Palabra Pupusa no es Pipil|trans-title=The Word Pupusa is Not Pipil|language=es|date=September 2016|access-date=6 July 2023|first1=Jorge E.|last1=Lemus|publisher=Don Bosco University}}</ref>{{Rp|1–5}}

== Origin ==
El Salvador and Honduras both claim to be the origin of the pupusa. Roque attributed the pupusa's origin to the [[Pipil people]] as he claims the term derived from a Nawat word, and he claims that a Nawat word would make El Salvador the pupusa's origin. Héctor Miguel Leiva Carías, a professor at the [[Universidad Nacional Autónoma de Honduras|National Autonomous University of Honduras]], thinks that Roque's reliance on word origin to determine food origin was not correct, as Nawat was also spoken in Honduras.<ref name="Univision" /> Salvadoran anthropologist Ramón Rivas does not attribute the pupusa to any country in particular except to state that it originated in [[Mesoamerica]] in general.<ref name="EM">{{cite web|url=https://diario.elmundo.sv/nacionales/donde-surgio-la-primera-pupusa-salvadorena|title=¿Dónde Surgió la Primera Pupusa Salvadoreña?|trans-title=Where Did the First Salvadoran Pupusa Originate?|language=es|date=13 November 2022|access-date=9 July 2025|work=[[El Mundo (El Salvador)|El Mundo]]|first1=Dennis|last1=Acosta}}</ref>

The pupusa's origin was discussed during negotiations for the [[Dominican Republic–Central America Free Trade Agreement]] (CAFTA–DR) in 2003.<ref name="Univision" /> El Salvador wanted to patent the pupusa as an exclusive export. Honduran negotiator Melvin Redondo ceded the right to El Salvador, stating that Honduras did not intend to challenge El Salvador on the matter.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.elsalvador.com/noticias/2003/12/08/nacional/nacio4.html|title=Honduras Insiste con Pupusas|trans-title=Honduras Insists with Pupusas|date=8 December 2003|access-date=8 July 2025|work=[[El Diario de Hoy]]|first1=Iván|last1=Gómez|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20041031230137/http://www.elsalvador.com:80/noticias/2003/12/08/nacional/nacio4.html|archive-date=31 October 2004|url-status=dead}}</ref> In 2018, the [[World Trade Organization]] listed El Salvador as the pupusa's {{lang|es|[[denominación de origen]]}} ("designation of origin").<ref name="LPG" />

== History ==
[[File:Apopa 1990 16.jpg|thumb|right|alt=A black-and-white photograph of a woman holding a basket of pupusas|A woman with pupusas in [[Apopa]] in 1990]]
[[File:La pupusa mas grande El Salvador.jpg|thumb|right|alt=A photograph of people cooking a 5.5 meter-wide pupusa|A {{convert|5.5|m|ft|sp=us|adj=mid|-wide}} pupusa created in [[Antiguo Cuscatlán]] in 2016]]

The earliest ancestral dish of the pupusa originated in the [[Pre-Columbian era]] in modern-day El Salvador, Honduras, and Guatemala as a thick corn [[tortilla]].<ref name="Univision" /> Franciscan friar [[Bernardino de Sahagún]] wrote in 1570 that Indigenous Americans prepared a dish consisting of a mixture of dough, beans, and meat in Pre-Columbian Mesoamerica.<ref name="EM" /><ref name="EDH">{{cite web|url=https://www.elsalvador.com/vida/gastronomia/pupusas-el-salvador-gastronomia/969486/2022/|title=Cinco Peculiares Datos que Quizás no Sabías Sobre las Pupusas|trans-title=|language=es|date=21 June 2022|access-date=9 July 2025|work=[[El Diario de Hoy]]|first1=L.|last1=Puente}}</ref>

The earliest mention of modern pupusas was made by Guatemalan poet [[José Batres Montúfar]] in 1837. In a letter to his family, he described encountering a dish similar to pupusas in [[Nicaragua]] known as {{lang|es|rellenas}}. The letter remarked their similarity to pupusas, which he attributes as being from [[San Salvador]].<ref name="LPG1">{{cite web|url=https://www.laprensagrafica.com/elsalvador/Un-plato-modesto-convertido-en-referente-cultural-20221112-0055.html|title=Un Plato Modesto Convertido en Referente Cultural|trans-title=A Modest Dish Turned into a Cultural Reference|language=es|date=13 November 2022|access-date=9 July 2025|work=[[La Prensa Gráfica]]|first1=Lissette|last1=Mondragón}}</ref> The pupusa was also mentioned by Honduran Alberto Membreño in ''Diccionario de Hondureñismos'' where he described it as an [[empanada]] "composed of cheese, beans, etc., enclosed in a tortilla and cooked on a [[Comal (cookware)|comal]]" ("{{lang|es|escompuesto de queso, frijoles, etc., encerrado en una tortilla y cocido en el comal}}").<ref name="Lemus" /> At this time, pupusas were most commonly eaten by the poor<ref name="LPG1" /> and prepared in rural areas.<ref name="BBC">{{cite web|url=https://www.bbc.com/travel/article/20190711-for-salvadorans-pupusas-mean-comfort|title=For Salvadorans, Pupusas Mean Comfort|language=en|date=11 July 2019|access-date=14 May 2022|work=[[BBC]]|first1=Laura|last1=Kiniry|first2=Daniel|last2=Kavanaugh}}</ref> In the late 1930s, corn became scarce and some Salvadorans replaced the corn flour to make pupusas with [[rice flour]]. This method of creating pupusas, known as {{lang|es|pupusas de arroz}}, began in [[Olocuilta]].<ref name="LPG" /> {{lang|es|Pupusas de arroz}} further spread during the [[Salvadoran Civil War]] due to further corn scarcity.<ref name="EM" /> The popularity of pupusas grew in the [[United States]] as refugees began preparing the dish in the country.<ref name="LPG1" />

''[[Guinness World Records]]'' has recognized a records for the largest pupusa created. {{As of|2024}}, the record belongs to a {{convert|6.1|m|ft|sp=us|adj=mid|-wide}} pupusa made in Washington, D.C.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.washingtonpost.com/dc-md-va/2024/09/28/pupusa-guinness-world-record-dc/|title=Salvadoran Chefs in D.C. Break World Record for Largest Pupusa|language=en|date=28 September 2024|access-date=30 September 2024|newspaper=[[The Washington Post]]|first1=Emma|last1=Uber}}</ref>

== Preparation ==
[[File:Festival del buen vivir y Gobernando con la gente-Ilobasco (26446524486).jpg|thumb|right|alt=A photograph of a woman cooking pupusas|Pupusas being cooked on a [[Comal (cookware)|comal]]]]

A pupusa is a handmade [[maize]] or [[rice]] tortilla stuffed with ingredients. Traditional stuffings can include cheese, [[refried beans]], squash, [[Echites panduratus|loroco]], and {{lang|es|[[chicharrón]]}}.<ref name="Perez de Novoa" /><ref name="BBC" /> Variants can also be stuffed with [[shrimp]], [[Chicken as food|chicken]], [[avocado]]s, or [[jalapeño]] peppers, among other ingredients.<ref name="EM" /> Pupusas can be served alongside {{Lang|es|[[curtido]]}} (a type of spicy [[coleslaw]]), [[tomato sauce]], [[Cooking banana|plantains]], [[tamale]]s, and vegetables.<ref name="TG" /><ref name="LAT">{{Cite news|url=https://www.latimes.com/archives/la-xpm-1990-08-09-fo-0-story.html|title=Pupusas|language=en|date=9 August 1990|access-date=13 May 2022|work=[[Los Angeles Times]]|first1=Barbara|last1=Hansen}}</ref> Pupusas are cooked on [[Comal (cookware)|comals]]<ref name="Lemus" /> and are traditionally eaten by hand.<ref name="LAT" /> According to the [[Food and Agriculture Organization]], each pupusa averages around 350 calories.<ref name="EDH" /> Locations that specialize in the preparation of pupusas are known as {{lang|es|pupuserías}}.<ref name="BBC" /> Pupusas can also be served as [[street food]].<ref name="TG">{{cite web|url=https://www.theguardian.com/travel/2011/sep/26/new-york-best-street-food|title=The Best Street Food in New York|date=26 September 2011|access-date=8 July 2025|work=[[The Guardian]]|first1=Perry|last1=Resnick}}</ref>

== Cultural significance ==

=== El Salvador ===
[[File:2010.05.04.165724 Pupusería Las Chinamas El Salvador.jpg|thumb|right|alt=A photograph of a woman cooking pupusas|A woman making pupusas in [[Ahuachapán]].]]
[[File:Pupuserías in Olocuilta at night.jpg|thumb|right|alt=A photograph of various pupuserías at night|{{lang|es|Pupuserías}} in [[Olocuilta]], nicknamed the "city of pupusas"]]

In El Salvador, the pupusa's cultural importance grew significantly during the Salvadoran Civil War as pupusas spread to and became popular in the United States in the 1980s.<ref name="LPG" /><ref name="LPG1" /> On 20 April 2005, the [[Legislative Assembly of El Salvador]] approved Decree 655 that declared the second Sunday of November to be National Pupusa Day ({{lang|es|Día Nacional de las Pupusas}}).<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.asamblea.gob.sv/decretos/details/1535|title=Declárase Día Nacional de las Pupusas|trans-title=Declaring the National Pupusas Day|language=es|date=20 April 2005|access-date=29 May 2020|work=[[Legislative Assembly of El Salvador]]|archive-date=6 August 2020|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200806150021/https://www.asamblea.gob.sv/decretos/details/1535|url-status=dead}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.cultura.gob.sv/secultura-invita-a-celebrar-el-dia-nacional-de-la-pupusa/|title=Secultura Invita al Día Nacional de la Pupusa|trans-title=Secultura Invites to National Pupusa Day|language=es|date=2018|access-date=29 May 2020|work=Ministry of Culture|archive-date=23 June 2018|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180623144409/http://www.cultura.gob.sv/secultura-invita-a-celebrar-el-dia-nacional-de-la-pupusa/|url-status=dead}}</ref> November was chosen as it marks the peak of corn harvesting in El Salvador.<ref name="LPG" />

Pupusa sales play a significant role in the [[Economy of El Salvador|Salvadoran economy]]. According to the Ministry of Economy, {{lang|es|pupuserías}} generated US$22 billion in revenue between 2001 and 2003.<ref name="EP">{{cite web|url=http://www.elperiodista.com.sv/index.php/45-municipios-activos/804-las-pupusas-icono-culinario-salvadoreno-celebrara-su-dia.html|title=Las Pupusas: Ícono Culinario Salvadoreño Celebrará Su Día|trans-title=Pupusas: Salvadoran Culinary Icon To Celebrate Its Day|language=es|date=4 November 2014|access-date=9 May 2015|work=El Periodista|archive-date=18 May 2015|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20150518095746/http://www.elperiodista.com.sv/index.php/45-municipios-activos/804-las-pupusas-icono-culinario-salvadoreno-celebrara-su-dia.html|url-status=dead}}</ref> In 2004, the Salvadoran Chamber of Consulting Entrepreneurs estimated that Salvadorans consumed around US$1.6 million worth of pupusas each weekend.<ref name="LPG" /><ref name="LPG1" /> By 2005, around 300,000 people made pupusas for a living, with a majority of them being women.<ref name="EP" />

The city of Olocuilta is nicknamed the "city of pupusas" ("{{lang|es|ciudad de las pupusas}}").<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.elsalvador.com/entretenimiento/turismo/cafe-comida-platillos-salvadorenos-pupusas-revueltas-/1102077/2023/|title=Pupusas Salvadoreñas: Sabores y Combinaciones Exóticas|trans-title=Salvadoran Pupusas: Exotic Flavors and Combinations|language=es|date=11 November 2023|access-date=28 July 2025|work=[[El Diario de Hoy]]|first1=Ricardo|last1=Vázquez|first2=Manuel|last2=Cruz}}</ref> Olocuilta has four locations known as {{lang|es|pupusódromos}} where several {{lang|es|pupuserías}} are located in close proximity with each other.<ref name="LPG" /> Salvadoran director Héctor Mojica produced a miniseries titled ''Las Pupusas'' about the dish. It was released on [[Amazon Prime Video]] in 2022 and 2023.<ref name="LPG1" /><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.primevideo.com/-/es/detail/Las-Pupusas/0FSF9FQV6NVN3M761LFY25PIJB|title=Las Pupusas|trans-title=The Pupusas|language=es|access-date=9 July 2025|work=[[Amazon Prime Video]]}}</ref>

=== United States ===
Pupusas spread to the United States during the 20th century when Central American immigrants moved to the country to escape violence in their home countries and opened {{lang|es|pupuserías}} in the U.S.<ref name="BBC" /> Pupusas are commonly eaten in cities such as [[Los Angeles]],<ref name="LAT" /> [[San Francisco]],<ref name="BBC" /> [[Washington, D.C.]],<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.nbcwashington.com/news/local/the-pupusa-iconic-salvadoran-dish-is-a-dc-mainstay-national-pupusa-day/2106358/|title=Praising the Pupusa: DC's Love for an Iconic Salvadoran Dish|language=en|date=8 November 2019|access-date=14 May 2022|work=[[WRC-TV]]|first1=Sophia|last1=Barnes|first2=Mariela|last2=Patron}}</ref><ref>{{cite journal|last1=Rodríguez|first1=Ana Patricia|date=2016|title=Becoming "Wachintonians": Salvadorans in the Washington, D.C., Metropolitan Area|language=en|journal=[[Washington History]]|publisher=[[Historical Society of Washington, D.C.]]|volume=28|issue=2|pages=3–12|issn=1042-9719|jstor=washhist.28.2.3|oclc=9970280163}}</ref>{{Rp|6}} and [[New York City]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2019/06/03/dining/ricas-pupusas-review.html|title=Eat Pupusas by Hand, Salvadoran Style, in Queens|website=[[The New York Times]] |language=en|date=3 June 2019|access-date=27 August 2025}}</ref> ''[[The Guardian]]'' named pupusas the 2011 Best Street Food in New York City.<ref name="TG" /> [[Taco Cabana]], a [[Tex-Mex]] chain in [[Texas]], created a dish called the ''pupusa'' that consists of chicken strips in a corn tortilla. It has no relation to the Central American dish.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.chron.com/culture/main/article/bona-fide-pupusas-1522608.php|title=Bona Fide Pupusas: Classic or Clueless? Here's How to Tell|language=en|date=27 July 2006|access-date=8 July 2025|work=[[Chron.com|Chron]]|first1=Eyder|last1=Peralta}}</ref>
