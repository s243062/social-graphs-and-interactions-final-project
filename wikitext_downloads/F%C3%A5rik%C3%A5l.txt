'''Fårikål''' ({{IPA|no|ˈfòːrɪkɔɫ, ˈfɔ̀rːɪkɔɫ}}) is a traditional [[Norwegian cuisine|Norwegian]] dish and the country's [[national dish]].<ref name=":0" /><ref name=":1" /><ref name=":2">{{Cite web |date=2023-01-19 |title=Norway's National Dish Explained - The Norway Guide |url=https://thenorwayguide.com/norways-national-dish/ |access-date=2023-03-22 |website=thenorwayguide.com |language=en-US}}</ref> It consists of pieces of [[Lamb and mutton|mutton]] with bone, [[cabbage]], whole [[black pepper]], and occasionally a little [[wheat flour]], cooked for several hours in a casserole, traditionally served with [[potato]]es boiled in their skins. The dish is typically prepared in early autumn, and over 70% of the Norwegian population reports eating the dish at least once during this time period.<ref>{{Cite web |title=Fårikål - Norges nasjonalrett holder stand {{!}} Opplysningskontoret for frukt og grønt |url=https://kommunikasjon.ntb.no/pressemelding/farikal---norges-nasjonalrett-holder-stand?publisherId=89251&releaseId=17941794 |access-date=2023-03-22 |website=kommunikasjon.ntb.no |language=no}}</ref><ref name=":2" />

Fårikål Feast Day is celebrated on the last Thursday in September each year.<ref name="farikal1">{{cite web|url=http://www.matprat.no/artikkel.aspx?artid=14544 |archive-url=https://archive.today/20130223004414/http://www.matprat.no/artikkel.aspx?artid=14544 |url-status=dead |archive-date=2013-02-23 |title=Fårikålens Festdag |publisher=Matprat.no |date=2010-10-04 |accessdate=2010-10-04 }}</ref><ref name="farikal2">{{cite web |url=http://www.farikal.no/om-oss/farikalens-festdag2/ |title=Fårikålens Festdag |publisher=Farikal.no |date=2010-10-04 |access-date=2010-10-04 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20101004190556/http://www.farikal.no/om-oss/farikalens-festdag2/ |archive-date=2010-10-04 }}</ref><ref name=":2" />

==Name==
Fårikål is a compound word literally meaning "mutton in cabbage".<ref name=":2" /> The name was amended from Danish "gaas i hvidkaal" (goose in white cabbage).

==In popular culture==
On September 29, 2012, [[Guinness World Records]] approved the World Record of making the largest portion of fårikål ever. The result was 594.2&nbsp;kg fårikål, prepared to be finished at the same time, consisting of 60% [[lamb and mutton|lamb]] and 40% [[cabbage]]. The event happened in [[Spikersuppa]], [[Oslo]], [[Norway]], and there were 10,000 guests present.<ref>{{cite web|title=Satte verdensrekord i fårikål |url=http://www.handelsbladetfk.no/id/26707 |publisher=Handelsbladet |access-date=18 October 2012 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20130921060600/http://www.handelsbladetfk.no/id/26707 |archive-date=21 September 2013 }}</ref>

In the 1970s, fårikål was elected [[national dish]] of Norway by the popular radio programme ''Nitimen''. In 2014, after the controversial decision by the food and agriculture minister [[Sylvi Listhaug]] to hold a new competition,<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.na-weekly.com/food/norways-national-dish-to-change/|title=Norway's national dish to change|date=23 May 2014|website=Na-weekly.com – The Norwegian American|accessdate=15 December 2017}}</ref><ref name=":1">{{cite web|url=http://www.thelocal.no/20140120/norway-to-replace|title=Norway to replace fårikål as national dish|date=20 January 2014|website=Thelocal.no|accessdate=15 December 2017}}</ref> it was reconfirmed as the national dish.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.newsinenglish.no/2014/06/16/farikal-wins-again-as-norways-national-dish/|title='Fårikål' wins again as Norway's national dish|website=Newsinenglish.no|date=16 June 2014 |accessdate=15 December 2017}}</ref><ref name=":0">{{cite web|url=http://www.thelocal.no/20140617/norway-replaces-frikl-as-national-dishwith-frikl|title=Norway sticks with fårikål as national dish|date=17 June 2014|website=Thelocal.no|accessdate=15 December 2017}}</ref>
